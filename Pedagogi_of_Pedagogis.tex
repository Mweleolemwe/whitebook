a\ominus b := a\,b^{-1}\in\Rpos,\qquad \ln(a\ominus b)=\ln a-\ln b.

\[
\slog_\epsilon(x)=\ln(x+\epsilon),\qquad \epsilon=\text{1\% of median nonzero (report)}.

\subsection{Harmonic ledger and low-variance music}

Equal-tempered pitches can be written as
\[
f_k = f_0\,2^{k/12},\qquad k\in\mathbb{Z},
\]
so that in the log-gauge $s=\ln f$ we have
\[
s_k = \ln f_k = \ln f_0 + \frac{k}{12}\ln 2.
\]
A semitone is a fixed step
\[
\Delta s_{\text{semi}} = \frac{\ln 2}{12},
\]
and any interval is an integer multiple of $\Delta s_{\text{semi}}$.
For a perfect fifth, $r=3/2$:
\[
\ln\!\frac{3}{2} \approx 7\,\Delta s_{\text{semi}} \quad (\text{mod } \ln 2),
\]
so ``up a fifth'' is ``plus seven semitone-steps in $s$ (mod octave).''

On a unit circle we assign to pitch index $k$ an angle
\[
\theta_k = \frac{2\pi k}{12},\qquad k\in\{0,\dots,11\},
\]
so the standard Circle of Fifths is the orbit of the map
\[
T_7(k) = k+7 \pmod{12}.
\]
In the Onu ledger picture this is a discrete linear flow on the $s$-lattice: repeated fifth steps walk a low-variance path through the spectrum, visiting a finite orbit while preserving the basic spectral envelope. Harmonic consonance corresponds to spectra where $P(f)$ is concentrated at such structured frequencies, yielding small $\Var_s[\ln P(f)]$.
